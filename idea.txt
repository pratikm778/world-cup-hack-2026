Pitch (60 seconds)
Name candidates: EdgeCast, PitchSide, LineWatch, NinetyMinute. Pick one early; everything else gets easier when you can name your product. I'll use EdgeCast below.

The pitch:

Polymarket has 200+ live markets during a single World Cup match — match winner, next goal scorer, exact score, corners, cards, half-time leader, minute of first goal. No human can watch all of them. Most bettors stare at one market and miss the moves on the other 199.

EdgeCast is a live betting copilot. It watches every market on the board, listens to the match commentary, and tells you in real time which markets are moving, which are about to move, and what to do about your open positions. It runs the analysis a sharp human would run on every market simultaneously, every minute of the match.

Today we're going to replay the final 25 minutes of Argentina-France 2022 — actual events, actual historical Polymarket data — and show you what EdgeCast would have told you, second by second, as it happened.

That's the framing. Don't oversell it as "AI predicts outcomes" — sell it as "AI keeps up with what a human couldn't."

Core features (build these, in this order)
1. Replay engine (foundation, build first — 4 hours)
Two synchronized streams playing back at configurable speed (1x for demo realism, 4x for development/testing):

Commentary stream: minute-by-minute match commentary as text chunks. Use a real match's commentary scraped from BBC/ESPN/Wikipedia minute-by-minute, augmented with Gemini-generated texture where it's thin.
Market data stream: time-synced snapshots of Polymarket prices for every market in that match, at 1-minute fidelity (pulled in advance via /prices-history and stored locally).
A "minute clock" UI element ticks visibly. Both streams advance together. This is the spine of the demo — if this is brittle, everything else is brittle.

2. Event classifier (the "fast hot path" — 4 hours)
A small fine-tuned model on GMI Cloud that takes each commentary chunk and emits a structured event:

{type: "goal" | "shot_on_target" | "shot_off_target" | "yellow_card" | 
       "red_card" | "substitution" | "corner" | "free_kick_danger" | 
       "save" | "injury" | "momentum_shift" | "vac",
 player: "Mbappé", team: "France", minute: 80, confidence: 0.94}
For the hackathon, you can absolutely skip fine-tuning and use Gemini Flash with a structured-output prompt — but the narrative of "we use GMI for the hot path because Gemini Flash is too slow/expensive at this rate" is your architectural story. Decide based on time: if you have 6 hours to spare, fine-tune a small model on GMI; otherwise use Flash and lie a little in the pitch about the production architecture.

3. Market relevance engine (the core feature — 6 hours)
This is the actual product. When an event fires, identify which of the 200 markets are most affected. Two-stage:

Semantic match: vector search over market titles + descriptions to find candidates relevant to the event ("red card to French defender" → "France wins," "Argentina wins," "next goal scorer," "total goals over 2.5," "cards over 4.5," etc.)
Price velocity check: cross-reference candidates against actual price movement in the last N minutes — markets that moved more get ranked higher.
Output: ranked list of 3-5 markets with movement %, current price, and a 1-line agent explanation.

This is the RocketRide showpiece: event → fan-out to embedding lookup + price velocity calc → ranker → Gemini synthesis. Pipeline graph judges can literally see.

4. Chat agent (the user-facing voice — 4 hours)
Gemini-powered chat panel that:

Auto-posts agent observations as they fire ("80': Mbappé equalizer. 'France wins' jumped from 28c to 41c. 'Goes to extra time' moved 19→34c — still room if you believe it.")
Accepts user questions ("why is the corners market not moving?" / "should I cash out my Argentina position?")
Has access to the full event history + current market state + user's open positions when answering
Keep this conversational and short. No paragraphs. Sharp, trader-style messages.

5. Position tracker (sidebar — 3 hours)
User clicks a "I bet on X" button next to any market. It appears in a "My Positions" sidebar section showing entry price, current price, P&L. Agent occasionally chimes in with hedge/cashout suggestions when a position's market moves materially.

Keep this tiny. One feature: track positions, surface relevant agent commentary. No order entry, no portfolio analytics, no allocation logic. Resist the urge.

6. UI layout (3 hours)
Three panes:

Left (40%): chat feed + input box. Agent messages with color-coded urgency (yellow = movement, red = big movement, blue = informational, green = your-position update).
Center (35%): video clip playing muted on loop (pure visual anchor, no AI processing) + the minute clock prominently displayed beneath it + scrolling commentary text below the video.
Right (25%): market sidebar. Top section: "Hot Markets" — the 5 most-moved in the last 2 minutes, glowing yellow when fresh. Bottom section: "My Positions" — the user's tracked bets.

Good instinct to refine this — the "what does the user actually do with this thing" question is the most under-specified part of your idea right now, and it'll determine whether the demo lands.

Let me lay out the choices, then make a recommendation.

The fundamental tension
You're trying to combine three different product modes, and they pull in different directions:

Mode A — Autonomous broadcaster: Agent talks, you watch. Like a Bloomberg ticker or a sports broadcast. You don't interact much; you absorb. Demo strength: high (judges watch agent reacting live). Demo weakness: feels passive, "where's the AI?" critique.

Mode B — Conversational analyst: You ask, agent answers. Like ChatGPT for betting. Demo strength: showcases reasoning depth. Demo weakness: judges have to watch you type, demo feels slow, every question is a chance for the model to say something dumb.

Mode C — Personal portfolio manager: Agent watches your positions specifically and advises. Demo strength: emotionally resonant ("the AI saved me money"). Demo weakness: requires a lot of fake setup, and the "manager" framing invites regulatory/ethical questions you don't want.

Most hackathon teams try to do all three and end up with three mediocre features. Pick one as primary, use one as secondary, cut the third.

My strong recommendation: Mode A primary, Mode B secondary, cut Mode C
Here's why, then I'll lay out what that means concretely.

Mode A (broadcaster) as primary because it's what no other product does well. Sportsbooks have odds. ChatGPT can answer questions. Nobody has an agent that watches everything for you and surfaces what matters. That's your differentiated wedge. And it's the most visually compelling — judges see the sidebar light up, see the chat fire alerts, feel the agent's pace.

Mode B (chat) as a "lean-in" feature — the user can interrupt the broadcast to ask follow-up questions, but the product works without it. This protects the demo: if your chat fails, the broadcast keeps going. If chat works, it's an "and also we have this" moment.

Cut Mode C (position tracking) for the demo, but keep the language. The pitch can mention "tracks your positions" as a roadmap item; the demo doesn't show it. This saves you ~6 hours of building a fake portfolio UI that distracts from the core.

What that means concretely — the refined core offering
The user experience in one sentence: "It's a live trading desk for a match. The agent narrates what's worth watching; you can interrupt to ask why."

Primary loop: the broadcast
The agent produces three kinds of messages, each visually distinct:

Movement alerts (~every 30-90 seconds, frequency varies with match activity). "82' — Corners Over 9.5 jumped 18→27c on France's third corner in 4 minutes. Pressure mounting." These are the bread and butter — they show the agent is watching things you'd miss.
Pre-event flags (rare but important — these are your wow moments). "78' — Argentina's high line + Mbappé's pace = mismatch building. 'Next goal: France' at 35c looks low given the last 4 minutes of territory." This is the "agent saw it coming" pitch. Hardest to build well, highest payoff.
State summaries (every 5-10 minutes, slow tempo). "85' Status: France equalizer reshuffled the board. Top 3 movers: extra time, total goals, France winner. Quietest markets: half-time leader (resolved), exact score." Gives the user a periodic "what's happening" without scrolling.
That's the broadcast. Three message types, distinct visual treatment, paced to feel alive but not spammy. That's the entire core feature.

Secondary loop: ask-the-agent
A chat input at the bottom of the broadcast. User can type questions, agent answers using full context (match state, all market prices, recent events, recent alerts).

What it's good for:

"Why did you flag the corners market?"
"What markets haven't moved that probably should?"
"If France equalizes, which markets move most?"
"Explain the price gap between Argentina win and France win — shouldn't it be tighter?"
What it's not for:

"Place a bet for me" (don't go there)
"What will happen next?" (don't promise prediction)
Long open-ended conversations (one-shot Q&A only)
Keep the design tight: chat is for interrogating the broadcast, not replacing it.

What I'd cut from your earlier list
Position tracking sidebar — cut. Add a single sentence in the pitch: "Production tracks your open positions and alerts on hedge opportunities." Don't build it.
"Should I cash out" suggestions — cut. Conflates the agent's job with portfolio advice. Same roadmap mention.
Bet placement / order entry — cut. Polymarket is real money; you don't want any UI suggesting actual order entry. This actively hurts the pitch because it raises liability questions.
What the user actually does during the demo
This is the test of any product spec — can you describe the user's behavior in 30 seconds?

The user opens EdgeCast at the start of the match. They glance at the screen periodically while watching the actual match elsewhere. The broadcast fires alerts; when something catches their eye, they read the explanation. If they want more depth on a particular call, they type a question. Otherwise they just watch the alerts roll in. When the match ends, they've absorbed a sharper picture of which markets moved and why than they would have from watching alone.

That's the offering. It's not "AI bets for you." It's not "ChatGPT for sports betting." It's "a market-aware co-watcher."

The refined feature list (replaces what's in your earlier draft)
Replay engine — same as before, this is the spine.
Event classifier — same as before.
The Broadcast (replaces "market relevance engine" + "chat agent" as separate features). One unified feature with three message types: movement alerts, pre-event flags, state summaries. Pacing logic is part of this — don't fire more than one message every ~20 seconds, prioritize by importance.
Ask-the-agent (chat input as secondary). Single-turn Q&A with full context.
Hot Markets sidebar — visual reinforcement of what the broadcast says, top 5 movers always visible.
Five features. Buildable. Each one demoable in under 15 seconds.

One more thing worth deciding now: tone of the broadcast
This actually matters more than people think. Three options:

Trader-terse: "82' Corners O9.5: 18→27c. Pressure +ve." Sharp, fast, sounds professional. Fits the "live trading desk" framing. Hardest to write well — needs consistent style.
Analyst-conversational: "Corners market is heating up — France just won their third corner in four minutes, and the over 9.5 line jumped from 18 to 27 cents." More accessible, easier to write, slightly slower feel.
Color-commentator: "France piling on the pressure! Corner count is climbing fast and the market noticed — over 9.5 is suddenly live." Most fun but risks sounding gimmicky and harder to keep useful.
I'd pick trader-terse with a sentence of context as the default. Looks like Bloomberg, reads like English: "82' — Corners O9.5: 18→27c. France's third corner in 4 minutes; pressure mounting." That's two lines per alert, scannable, sharp.
